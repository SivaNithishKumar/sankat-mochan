# 1. WAYANAD LANDSLIDES, KERALA, INDIA — July 30, 2024

## What Happened: Timeline

At **2:17 AM IST on July 30, 2024**, a massive landslide struck near the origin of the Punnappuzha river, between the plantation villages of Punjirimattom and Mundakkai in Wayanad district, Kerala. The villages — mostly home to tea and coffee estate workers, many of them Scheduled Tribe families — were built on hillsides in a documented landslide-prone zone.

At **4:10 AM**, a second larger landslide hit Chooralmala, diverting the course of the Iruvanjippuzha river and triggering flash floods that swept downstream through Vellarimala. The debris flow velocity reached 57 metres per second — approximately 130 mph. The National Remote Sensing Centre later determined that roughly 86,000 square metres of hillside — nearly 21 acres — had moved downhill in those two events.

In the hours that followed, 4,000+ people were rescued. But 10,000+ had to be relocated because their homes were gone or inaccessible. By the time official search operations closed on August 9, **420 people had died** (though earlier reports cited 254 — numbers revised upward as bodies were recovered from the Chaliyar River downstream). 17 entire families were killed in their entirety. 11 children were orphaned.

Three bridges were destroyed, 124 km of road were damaged, and the area was completely cut off. The Indian Army constructed a temporary 190-foot Bailey bridge in 31 hours to allow rescue vehicles in.

**Sources:** [Wikipedia — 2024 Wayanad Landslides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Wayanad_landslides) | [NDRF Operations Report](https://www.ndrf.gov.in/en/operations/landslide-wayanad-kerala)

## The People: Survivor Accounts

### Rose Diana, 36 — Saipukunnu, Nellimunda

A plantation worker living in a modest hillside shanty with her two sons (8 and 11):

> *"I felt the ground under me shaking in the night."*

She woke her children instantly, not knowing what was happening. The laterite embankment around her house began to collapse; the road was flooding. She prayed, grabbed her children, and navigated a steep, slippery forested path behind her house — a path used in daylight with warnings of leopards, elephants, and wild boar — to reach a neighbor's house. They survived by moving uphill through the forest, in darkness, without any alert having reached them.

Later: *"I prayed and I cried... I don't want to go back to my house."*

### Gauri, 62 — Chooralmala

She had lived through prior landslides but not this:

> *"I have seen many landslides. But this time, the entire mountains came down."*

After the first landslide, neighbors gathered at her house. When the second explosion of debris arrived, they fled into a nearby forest. Her grandson Vinay had a fractured kneecap and had to drag himself through the forest. Local men from the village guided the group through the darkness while also managing the presence of elephant herds in the forest. Later:

> *"I am not going back to my house even if it is standing there."*

### Prasanna Kumar, 50 — Poonchirimattom, welder

> *"We could see the debris flying before our eyes, 20 metres away. It was like a train but 150 feet high."*

He grabbed documents in a plastic bag and ran toward the road. The survivors of Poonchirimattom walked in the rain through the forest and behind the hill at Vellarimala — the very hill from which the landslide originated — to find a safe place. No vehicle, no warning, no authority told them where to go. They walked.

### Sahira Banu — estate worker

Struck by a log at 1 AM, causing head and ear injuries. Her relatives pulled her out of the dirt. Her husband disappeared and was never found. She later underwent head surgery. Her account illustrates a common pattern: entire households where one person survived and one did not, separated by which direction they fell.

### Joyson — tourist guide

When the debris arrived at his building, he took his family to the terrace. A concrete slab from an adjacent structure had collapsed in a way that created a deflection barrier, redirecting the water flow away from his position. He survived by chance geometry.

### Mary Thomas — widow, Mundakkai

Lost her husband, son, and their small coffee and pepper holding. She now receives periodic letters from banks:

> *"Every few weeks another letter comes. They remind me to pay for the land that no longer exists."*

Her late husband's papers sit in a box — the only material thing she kept. She receives a modest government rent allowance but fears being blacklisted by banks. Her son now works as a daily-wage laborer.

**Source:** [Down to Earth — "For Wayanad 2024's Survivors, the Landslide Never Ended"](https://www.downtoearth.org.in/natural-disasters/for-wayanad-2024s-survivors-the-landslide-never-ended-it-only-moved-from-the-slopes-to-the-ledgers) | [Onmanorama — Survivor Escape Accounts](https://www.onmanorama.com/news/kerala/2024/08/03/wayanad-how-survivors-traversed-through-biggest-landslide-of-their-lives.html)

**How people escaped:** The primary method of survival was self-evacuation on foot through forests and hillsides in complete darkness, guided by neighbors or alone. Organized rescue teams (Army, NDRF, fire, coast guard, Navy — 1,531 personnel total) rescued and evacuated further thousands after dawn. Helicopters were used for the most remote extractions. The community itself — through informal coordination, volunteer organizations (White Guard, DYFI, IYC), and the District Emergency Operation Center — handled much of the coordination.

## Communication and Warning Failures

This is documented and contested. The Indian Meteorological Department (IMD) and Geological Survey of India (GSI) did **not** issue specific landslide red alerts for Mundakkai and surroundings. The central government claimed early rainfall warnings were transmitted to the state; Kerala's Chief Minister disputed the adequacy of this.

Crucially: The **Hume Centre for Ecology and Wildlife Biology**, based in Wayanad, had warned the district administration about a potential landslide in Mundakkai and surrounding areas **16 hours before the event**. The district administration did not act on it because it was not integrated into the official warning system.

In the villages themselves: poor mobile connectivity in remote hillside plantations meant that even warnings that were issued could not reach residents. Most plantation workers had no access to automated alert systems. Many of the dead were Scheduled Tribe families whose homes were deepest in the hills, furthest from road access.

**Post-disaster analysis:** India's landslide early warning capacity is acknowledged by researchers as "insufficient" for localized events with complex triggering mechanisms. The Wayanad disaster accelerated discussion of dedicated multi-hazard warning systems for the Western Ghats.

**Sources:** [IIM Kozhikode — Early Warning System Analysis](https://iimk.ac.in/centres/IJCCC/docs/IJCCC-WayanadLandslide2024_EarlyWarningSystem.pdf) | [Down to Earth — Warning Systems](https://www.downtoearth.org.in/climate-change/wayanad-disaster-highlights-need-for-multi-hazard-early-warning-systems) | [Onmanorama — Lessons Learned](https://www.onmanorama.com/news/kerala/2024/12/10/wayanad-landslide-kerala-lessons-learnt-warning-systems-rehabilitation.html)

## The Discussion-Thread Layer

Social media response focused on:
- Frantic missing-person posts from diaspora Keralites trying to locate relatives in the affected hamlets (many estate workers had no connectivity to report their status)
- Donation coordination through WhatsApp chains — the Inter Agency Group and DEOC became coordination hubs, but grassroots youth organizations (DYFI, IYC, SYS, SKSSF) mobilized faster than official channels
- Subsequent political argument about who issued what warning to whom and when — a contested narrative still active in December 2024

**Long-term grief texture:** The financial aftermath has been particularly brutal. Families collectively owe Rs 35.3 crore to nationalized banks — loans for homes, farming equipment, education, and the land — that is now buried under 20 feet of debris. Kerala Bank wrote off Rs 4.98 crore. Other banks refused. Women's self-help groups that provided collective loan guarantees discovered that when five members die, the surviving members are pursued for the dead members' share — "collective guarantees became collective punishment," said Gracy Jose, an SHG leader in Chooralmala who lost five members.

**Source:** [Down to Earth](https://www.downtoearth.org.in/natural-disasters/for-wayanad-2024s-survivors-the-landslide-never-ended-it-only-moved-from-the-slopes-to-the-ledgers)

## Narrative Hooks for Presentation (Wayanad)

1. **The 16-hour warning that went nowhere.** An ecology research centre warned the district administration 16 hours before the landslide. It wasn't in the official system. 420 people died.
2. **Walking through the forest in the dark, with elephants.** Prasanna Kumar and his neighbors navigating forest paths at night, with wild animals, no phone signal, no headlights — just each other and knowledge of the hillside. This is how people actually survived.
3. **Paying loans for land that doesn't exist.** Mary Thomas receiving bank letters reminding her to repay for a coffee farm that is now under 20 feet of rock. The landslide kept happening in the ledgers.

# 2. VALENCIA / SPAIN DANA FLOODS — October 29, 2024

## What Happened: Timeline

DANA (Depresión Aislada en Niveles Altos — Isolated High-Altitude Depression) is a meteorological system common to the Spanish Mediterranean coast that can dump enormous rainfall in compressed timeframes. The October 2024 event was extraordinary in scale.

- **October 25**: Spanish meteorologists flagged potential high-impact conditions five days out.
- **October 29, 6:42 AM**: AEMET (national weather agency) issued orange warning for southern Valencia.
- **October 29, 7:36 AM**: Red alert issued for the Valencian interior.
- **October 29, 1:00 PM**: Regional President Carlos Mazón told a press conference that the storm "would dissipate by 6 PM."
- **October 29, 6:00 PM**: The town of Turís set a new Spanish rainfall record — **184.6 mm in one hour**. One year of rain fell in eight hours in some areas. In Chiva, **nearly 500 mm fell in eight hours** — one of the highest rainfall intensities ever recorded in Europe.
- **October 29, 6:30 PM**: The Poyo ravine burst its banks. The Horta Sud region — a densely populated network of commuter towns south of Valencia — was inundated within minutes. Many residents were in their cars, in rush-hour traffic, heading home from work.
- **October 29, 8:11 PM**: The ES-Alert cell phone warning was finally sent to Valencian citizens, telling them to remain indoors.

By 8:11 PM, most of the 238 people who died were already dead. The judicial investigation that followed confirmed that **the majority of deaths occurred between 7:00 PM and 8:00 PM** — in the hour before the warning was sent.

**The Japanese embassy comparison:** Acting on the same AEMET red alert, the Japanese embassy in Spain had notified its residents a full day in advance, advising them to avoid the area or remain indoors. The Generalitat Valenciana — which had the same data — waited 13 hours after the red alert to send citizen warnings.

**Sources:** [Wikipedia — 2024 Spanish Floods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Spanish_floods) | [PMC Research Article](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12066247/) | [GNDR — Failures in Early Warning](https://www.gndr.org/2024-spain-floods-early-warning-action-coordination-and-localisation/) | [The Leader — Delayed Alert Analysis](https://theleader.info/2025/02/28/blood-on-their-hands-the-tragic-cost-of-a-delayed-valencia-dana-alert/)

## The People: Survivor Accounts

### Leonel Cuevas Almendarez, 31 — Benetúser, Valencia
A bricklayer originally from Honduras, exhausted from 12-hour days at the port, who had taken the afternoon off. His account is the most vivid English-language first-person narrative available:

He had gone out with his partner and young son when the power cut out at home. Looking outside, the pavement was covered with water, though it wasn't raining in Benetúser — the water was coming from somewhere else entirely. He went downstairs; by the time he reached his car, water was at his knees. He managed to drive 20 metres before the engine stalled.

He could hear his son screaming in terror from their building's balcony above. He had to reach him.

> *"I began jumping from car to car, shedding my heavy fleece jacket and leaving the mace behind. When I ran out of cars, I plunged into the water, battling through the current as debris slammed into me."*

He covered approximately 20 metres through the torrent, fighting debris, before a neighbor pulled him inside the building entrance.

The next morning:

> *"A woman cried inconsolably nearby — the first sign suggesting people died."*

Every street had 50 cars piled on one another, wedged between tree trunks and up in branches. Pavements had been completely ripped up. Houses were missing walls. Debris was everywhere.

**Source:** [Orato World — Leonel's Account](https://orato.world/2024/11/26/floods-in-valencia-a-fathers-harrowing-journey-to-save-his-son-amid-chaos-and-destruction/)

### People Trapped in Underground Garages

One of the most lethal dynamics of the Valencia floods was uniquely counterintuitive: because it was **not raining** in many affected towns when the water arrived, residents went to garages to rescue their cars. Seven bodies were recovered from a single underground parking garage. 35 of the 238 dead were found in car parks and basements. **Going to protect property was a death sentence** — and no alert had warned them not to go down.

### The Highway Trap

The A-3 motorway runs through Horta Sud, parallel to the Poyo ravine. At peak rush hour, with the ravine bursting, hundreds of vehicles were on the highway. The Júcar Hydrographic Confederation estimated maximum flows of 3,500 cubic metres per second at the intersection of the ravine and the A-3. Witnesses described cars being lifted and swept downstream. People were seen standing on the roofs of their vehicles as the water rose. Some were carried miles.

## Communication and Coordination Failures

This is the defining story of Valencia 2024: a system with all the right technical tools, all the right data, and a political failure to activate them in time.

**What failed:**
1. The regional government had **disbanded the Valencian Emergency Unit in 2023**, which critics argued was the body designed to translate meteorological warnings into population-level action.
2. AEMET issued warnings beginning at 6:42 AM — over 13 hours before the citizen alert. The warnings were technically correct and available to officials.
3. Regional President Mazón's 1 PM press conference — publicly stating the storm would dissipate — gave a false signal of normalcy during peak warning hours.
4. The ES-Alert system (the Spanish equivalent of Wireless Emergency Alert) was not activated until **8:11 PM**, after the peak of deaths.
5. 112 emergency lines became immediately saturated; residents turned to social media to call for help and locate missing persons.
6. Catalan emergency teams offered assistance; they were initially rejected for political reasons.
7. Volunteer access to affected areas was subsequently restricted by regional authorities — the very volunteers who arrived before the Military Emergencies Unit.

**Criminal investigation:** A judge investigating the case stated that many deaths "could have been prevented with the correct warning message sent in time."

**Survivors' testimony to EU officials (May 2025):**
> *"We, the families of the victims, are not provided any psychological support to help us overcome this trauma. We don't have therapists, and we don't have any compensation for the deceased."*

**Source:** [Euronews — Survivors Brief EU](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2025/05/13/valencias-floods-victims-met-von-der-leyen-and-metsola-in-brussels) | [Euronews — Warning Expert Analysis](https://www.euronews.com/2024/11/07/early-warnings-save-lives-could-valencia-have-been-better-prepared-for-deadly-flooding)

## The Discussion-Thread Layer

**Real-time chaos:** When the water hit during rush hour, 112 was immediately overwhelmed. People trapped in cars, on rooftops, and in flooding buildings posted to X (Twitter) and WhatsApp group chats as their only means of calling for help. Many of these requests went unanswered for hours.

**Volunteer self-organization:** In the absence of coordinated government response, volunteers from across Spain organized through WhatsApp chains, social media groups, and self-created digital platforms:
- "Ajuda DANA Sistema Coordinación" — volunteer matching platform
- "Buscador DANA" — an all-in-one search engine for donors, volunteers, and victims
- "Zubi Help" — coordination managed by Zubi Group
- Emergency landmark maps built using WhatsApp and phone coordination

When the Mazón government tried to formalize volunteer effort on November 1, the attempt collapsed into "chaos and confusion" — volunteers disembarked from assigned government buses and walked to affected towns instead, returning to self-organization.

**Misinformation:** Bot accounts flooded X with misleading and violent content about the floods, complicating genuine information flows.

**Source:** [Wikipedia Spanish Floods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_Spanish_floods) | [Euronews — Bot Accounts](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2024/11/07/bot-accounts-flood-spanish-social-media-with-misleading-flood-claims)

## Narrative Hooks for Presentation (Valencia)

1. **The 8:11 PM alert — sent after the deaths.** The warning arrived at 8:11 PM. The deaths happened between 7:00 and 8:00 PM. This is not a hypothesis; it is in the judicial record.
2. **Jumping car-to-car through a torrent to reach your screaming child.** Leonel Cuevas's account is the most vivid documented human story from this disaster — because he survived, because it's sourced, and because it crystallizes what it feels like to be left without a warning.
3. **Going to the garage to save the car was a death sentence.** Because it wasn't raining in Benetúser, people went downstairs. 35 bodies were found in underground garages. The false sense of safety from no local rain was the trap — and a working alert would have broken it.

# 3. LOS ANGELES WILDFIRES — January 7–31, 2025

## What Happened: Timeline

On January 7, 2025, hurricane-force Santa Ana winds (100 mph in some areas) combined with extreme drought, critically low humidity, and months of accumulated dry vegetation to create catastrophic fire conditions across the Los Angeles basin. The National Weather Service had issued red flag warnings with "particularly dangerous situation" language.

- **10:30 AM, January 7**: Palisades Fire ignites near Pacific Palisades.
- **6:15 PM, January 7**: Eaton Fire reported in Eaton Canyon, near Altadena/Pasadena border. Starts at 20 acres.
- Within hours, both fires were explosive. By January 8, the Palisades had reached 5,000 acres; the Eaton had hit 10,600 acres.
- By January 9, nearly 50,000 customers had lost power. Multiple fires ignited across LA and Ventura counties simultaneously.
- Both fires were fully contained January 31, 2025.

**Total burned:** 57,529 acres. **Structures destroyed or damaged:** 18,189. **Deaths directly attributed:** 31 (12 Palisades, 19 Eaton). **Excess deaths from smoke, disrupted healthcare, and systemic stress** estimated at ~440 (Boston University/University of Helsinki, August 2025 study in JAMA).

**Sources:** [Wikipedia — January 2025 Southern California Wildfires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/January_2025_Southern_California_wildfires) | [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/event/Los-Angeles-wildfires-of-2025)

---

## The People: Survivor Accounts

### The Pickleball WhatsApp Chat That Became an Evacuation Network

Two years before the fire, a group of about 50 residents of the Altadena Town & Country Club had formed a WhatsApp group to arrange pickleball games. On January 7, 2025, before anyone received an official emergency alert about the Eaton Fire, a message lit up that chat:

> *"Everyone look up, there's a vegetation fire on Canyon Close. If you're anywhere near Eaton Canyon, I'd evacuate."*

For the next several days, the pickleball chat served as a real-time informal emergency network — evacuation alerts, safe-haven offers, updates on where the fire was moving, and status updates on what had been lost. It did what the official alert system did not do in time.

The formal alert for west Altadena did not arrive until **3:30 AM on January 8** — nine hours after the fire started, and almost 12 hours after an emergency management employee had urged county officials to pre-send an alert about the incoming wind conditions. Almost all 19 Eaton Fire deaths occurred in west Altadena.

**Source:** [NPR coverage / LA County confirmation](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/california-wildfires-what-we-know-victims-killed-rcna188240)

### Erion and Stephan Taylor — Altadena

Erion spotted the approaching fire and called her husband:

> *"My heart dropped, I saw the flames, and I said 'Honey, come here for a second.'"*

His demeanor immediately changed. They had 20 minutes to pack. They grabbed wedding photographs, loved ones' ashes, stuffed animals, soccer cleats. Drove seven minutes to his mother Peggy's house — then had to flee again when the fire reached there too.

> *"I don't care about things at all. Everything can be replaced. I have everything I need right here."*

They've been in a hotel room since. Their house is rubble.

**Source:** [ABC News](https://abcnews.com/US/altadena-family-recounts-harrowing-escape-eaton-fire/story?id=117656458)

### Rory Sykes, 32 — Malibu, Palisades Fire

Rory Sykes, born blind with cerebral palsy, was a former child TV star turned motivational speaker. He lived in a self-contained cottage on his mother Shelley's 17-acre Malibu estate when the Palisades Fire arrived.

Shelley Sykes saw embers on the roof of his cottage. She tried to extinguish them with a hose:

> *"There was no water coming out."*

She drove to the nearest fire station for help. They told her they had no water either. By the time she returned, Rory was unreachable. He died from carbon monoxide poisoning.

> *"He was my baby and he died needlessly."*

She told Australian television: *"I couldn't lift him. I couldn't move him."*

His death crystallizes the cascading failure: no preemptive alert, no water pressure, no response capacity, no way to help a resident who could not self-evacuate. (Note: Las Virgenes Municipal Water later disputed Shelley's account, stating water service was uninterrupted. The disputed claim remains unresolved.)

**Source:** [NBC News — Rory Sykes](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/former-child-star-rory-sykes-dies-california-wildfires-rcna187252) | [CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/la-fires-rory-sykes-killed-former-child-actor-malibu-mother-says/)

### Donny Kincey — Altadena

Evacuating his parents' house:

> Flames were *"pouring down the mountains behind him like liquid, and the sky had turned white from the incredible heat."*

He lost his house and ten years of artwork.

## Communication and Warning Failures

**Pre-warning failure:** On January 7, nearly two hours before the Eaton Fire ignited, an LA County emergency management employee pushed for a pre-fire alert warning residents about the extreme wind conditions. No alert was sent. The formal evacuation warning for west Altadena wasn't issued until 3:30 AM on January 8 — 12 hours later, 12 hours after the moment when advance warning might have made a difference.

**Water supply collapse:** LA's firefighting water infrastructure was designed for isolated residential fires, not hundreds of simultaneous burning homes. The Santa Ynez Reservoir (117 million gallons), the primary upper-Palisades pressure tank, had been emptied for repairs on January 7. Firefighters ran out of water as the Palisades Fire exploded.

**Predeployment failure:** Despite NWS extreme fire danger alerts days in advance, the city pre-deployed only 9 engines on January 6 and 8 more on January 7, across the entire city — with zero pre-deployed to the Palisades area.

**Budget cuts context:** LA Fire Chief Kristin Crowley had warned the city that $17.6 million in budget reductions "adversely affected the Department's ability to maintain core operations" and that $7 million in overtime cuts "severely limited the Department's capacity to prepare for, train for, and respond to large-scale emergencies."

**The mistaken countywide alert:** On January 9, an erroneous evacuation warning was issued for the entirety of Los Angeles County (10 million residents) instead of just the Kenneth Fire area — causing mass panic and confusion. LA County subsequently suspended the Genasys alert system.

**Source:** [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/January_2025_Southern_California_wildfires) | [AOL/Union Bulletin — Pre-Alert Decision](https://www.aol.com/articles/officials-considered-warning-altadena-eaton-100000142.html)

## The Discussion-Thread Layer

**Sierra Club/survivor networks:** Three survivors profiled by the Sierra Club documented parallel journeys — guilt at surviving, uncertainty about whether to rebuild, hope for community continuity. One described "survivor's guilt" toward neighbors who lost more.

**Eaton Fire Survivors Network:** Joy Chen's grassroots network became a model of post-disaster community leadership — starting as a rapid-response housing/clothing/needs coordination hub in the days after the fire, then expanding to insurance support, public accountability, and community identity preservation in Altadena. [California Community Foundation writeup](https://www.calfund.org/news-and-events/eaton-fire-survivors-network/)

**Altadena identity:** Altadena is a historically Black and working-class community adjacent to wealthy Pasadena. After the fire, concerns emerged about displacement pressure, rent-gouging (landlords violating California's price-gouging laws by raising rents 15-20% or doubling them during the emergency), and corporate buyout of burned land. Heavy metal contamination from burned structures (36% of Eaton burn-scar homes had elevated lead levels) added long-term health dimensions.

## Narrative Hooks for Presentation (LA Fires)

1. **The pickleball chat that saved lives while officials deliberated.** Before any formal warning, 50 people in a tennis club WhatsApp group knew about the fire and were evacuating each other. The county alert came 12 hours later.
2. **Rory Sykes: the man who couldn't self-evacuate and nobody could reach.** The failure is almost mechanical — no preemptive warning to prepare, no water when fire arrived, no capacity to respond to a call for help. A man who needed help could not get it.
3. **The empty reservoir.** The reservoir that should have pressurized fire hydrants across the Palisades was empty for routine repairs on the exact day of the worst fire in LA history.

# 4. MYANMAR EARTHQUAKE — March 28, 2025

## What Happened: Timeline

At **12:50 PM local time on March 28, 2025**, a magnitude 7.7–7.9 strike-slip earthquake struck Myanmar with its epicenter near Mandalay, the country's second-largest city. The rupture extended approximately 500 km. A 6.7 aftershock followed 12 minutes later. More aftershocks continued for days.

The earthquake struck during **Friday midday prayers** — a catastrophically timed coincidence. In Mandalay alone, all 40 mosques were damaged, 10 collapsed, killing over 400 worshippers. At the U Hla Thein Temple, 600+ monks were trapped when the hall collapsed during examinations; 80 died. Over 50 mosques nationwide collapsed during prayers; approximately 700 Muslim worshippers died in this single window.

Structural failures cascaded: a 12-story condominium in Mandalay "progressively collapsed," killing 207. A bank building in Pyawbwe Township collapsed, killing "all 40 people inside."

**Source:** [Wikipedia — 2025 Myanmar Earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Myanmar_earthquake) | [UN News](https://news.un.org/en/story/2025/03/1161716)

---

## The People: Survivor Accounts

### Ma Win Win, 38 — Ayeyarwady Region

During dinner, her husband ran to find their son as the earthquake struck. He slipped in the kitchen; bricks fell on him.

> *"My head felt like it was burning. Another big stone hit my head."*

Five family members were initially trapped. Her son was found alive after a five-hour excavation — sheltered in her sister's arms beneath rubble. Her sister and husband did not survive. Her wrist arteries were severed, requiring surgery; she cannot fully use her hand and cannot work. 

> *"I just want my husband back."*

Her young child experienced head injuries and ongoing fear.

### Khin Myo Khaing, 35

In the kitchen with visitors when the shaking began. She initially thought it was rain.

> *"I was so worried for my four-and-a-half-month-old baby in the other room."*

## Communication and Coordination: The Internet Shutdown Dimension

Myanmar's Sagaing Region — the epicenter — was already under a military-imposed internet blackout before the earthquake. The country experienced 85 documented internet shutdowns in 2024 alone, more than any other nation. The junta uses communications blackouts as a tool of military suppression.

**What this meant in practice:**

- "In areas with complete blackouts, residents have had to **travel for several days** to get connectivity to call in their needs to aid groups." (Humanitarian worker, Context/TRF)
- Aid took three days to reach one town due to lack of coordination capabilities.
- Most of the Burmese population remained uninformed about the earthquake's extent for **more than 24 hours**.
- An activist in Sagaing could not confirm the fate of two friends in Mandalay for over a week.
- Starlink terminals — which could have provided emergency connectivity — are not licensed in Myanmar. A few aid organizations flew in their own Starlink devices; terminals cost $389 to start. Others paid $0.48 for a two-hour internet session to get basic survival information, while average daily wages are $2.20.

**Digital rights advocate Htaike Htaike Aung:**
> *"Internet shutdowns are a matter of life and death during this time of crisis."*

> *"Even though people might have access to Starlink, they can't necessarily use it in this situation because it's not legal."*

**Justice For Myanmar:** Called the situation a "digital dictatorship denying lifesaving information access."

**Self-rescue:** Due to limited professional equipment and emergency specialists, Mandalay's residents conducted their own rescue with bare hands. The Mandalay Fire Services Department rescued 403 people and recovered 259 bodies by March 31 — but that was a fraction of the full scale. In Taungoo (Bago Region), nearly a week after the earthquake, residents were still self-rescuing with inadequate tools because no international assistance had arrived.

**The junta's interference:** Despite the catastrophe, Myanmar's military continued to conduct airstrikes — "at least 72" — in subsequent weeks, killing over 100 additional people. Human rights organizations documented selective allocation of aid, with resistance-held regions receiving markedly less support. This was not a natural disaster purely; it was a natural disaster filtered through a dictatorship.

# 5. TEXAS HILL COUNTRY / CAMP MYSTIC FLOODS — July 4, 2025

## What Happened: Timeline

Central Texas's Hill Country is "Flash Flood Alley" — limestone bedrock, shallow soil, steep slopes that drain rainwater into rivers in minutes rather than hours. The Guadalupe River has flooded catastrophically before. A 1987 flood killed 10 people at Camp Mystic; the camp was rebuilt, expanded, and eventually appealed its flood zone designation until FEMA removed ~30 buildings from the official flood hazard maps.

On **July 3, 2025**, severe thunderstorms stalled over the Hill Country. By evening, the National Weather Service issued a flood watch for Kerr County with language warning of "considerable to catastrophic flash flood impacts."

- **1:14 AM, July 4**: First flash flood warning for central Kerr County.
- **1:14 AM, July 4**: Camp Mystic director Richard "Dick" Eastland received this warning.
- **2:30 AM, July 4**: Eastland began evacuation — **76 minutes after receiving the warning**.
- The Guadalupe River rose **26 feet in 45 minutes**, cresting at 37.52 feet — a new record. At the peak, **5.22 inches of rain per hour** fell over the watershed. The equivalent of four months of rain fell in a few hours.
- **4:03 AM**: Flash flood emergency declared for south-central Kerr County.
- **4:22 AM**: A volunteer firefighter, an hour into the flood response, radioed dispatch asking if CodeRED (Kerr County's mass alert system) could be activated. Dispatcher: *"Stand by, we have to get that approved with our supervisor."*
- **5:34 AM**: The first CodeRED alert finally went out — **nearly 90 minutes** after the firefighter's request. Some messages took nearly six hours, arriving at 10 AM.
- By morning: **27 campers and counselors at Camp Mystic were dead**, including director Eastland. At least 139 people died across five Texas counties; the final toll reached 137 confirmed.

The NWS issued 22 escalating alerts over July 3-4 for Kerr County, including explicit language about "catastrophic flood damage" and "a large and deadly flood wave." No local government alert was issued in time.

**Sources:** [Wikipedia — July 2025 Central Texas Floods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/July_2025_Central_Texas_floods) | [Texas Tribune — Camp Mystic](https://www.texastribune.org/2025/07/07/texas-hill-country-flooding-camp-mystic/) | [Texas House Report on Camp Mystic (June 2026)](https://www.house.texas.gov/pdfs/committees/355/Report-on-the-Camp-Mystic-Flood-Disaster-of-July-4-2025.pdf)

## The People: Survivor Accounts

Approximately 30 minutes after the flooding overwhelmed the camp, director Eastland and several girls were swept out and reached a tree, where they clung to its branches through the night.

Some campers tried to grasp the branches and were torn away by the current. Others were swept past without being able to reach the tree at all.

At least one group of girls managed to find safety in a grove of trees, where they held on until rescue teams reached them.

## The Comfort Contrast

Twenty miles away, in Kendall County's town of Comfort, **zero people died**.

Comfort had upgraded its disaster alert system in 2024, adding a new siren (a nonprofit funded $60,000; Bandera Electric donated poles). The system was linked to USGS water gauges. On July 4, at **10:52 AM**, seven minutes after the river began rising near Comfort, both sirens sounded.

This was the first time the two-siren system was used outside a test.

Nobody died.

**Kerr County had no siren system.** County officials had debated installing one for years. Grant requests in 2017 and 2018 were denied. American Rescue Plan funds were available after 2021 but not allocated to flood systems. Some county residents opposed funding over concerns about federal mandates including COVID vaccination requirements. Kerr County Judge Rob Kelly, at his July 4 press conference:

> *"We have no warning system. We didn't know this flood was coming."*

The Camp Mystic girls were prohibited from bringing cell phones to camp. Even if CodeRED had worked, it would not have reached them.

## Communication and Warning Failures

The systemic failures here are documented in the June 2026 Texas House investigative report and are unusually specific:

1. **Camp Mystic had no radios for counselors.** No walkie-talkies, no communication system — teen counselors with no emergency training were expected to react to a flash flood at 2 AM with no tools.
2. **76-minute delay in camp evacuation** after director received the flash flood warning. The Texas House report concluded deaths were preventable.
3. **Kerr County never activated its more powerful EAS system.** FEMA records confirm this.
4. **CodeRED required supervisor authorization** — at 4:22 AM, during an active flooding emergency, a dispatcher told a volunteer firefighter to wait for approval. Authorization came 90 minutes later.
5. **Cell phone alerts have structural limits** in rural areas: poor signal, phones off at night, campers prohibited from bringing phones.
6. **NWS staffing vacancies**: The San Angelo NWS office had multiple vacancies including the meteorologist-in-charge. San Antonio had no warning coordination meteorologist. Vacancy rates had roughly doubled since January 2025.
7. **FEMA deployment delayed**: Secretary Noem's requirement that she personally sign off on all contracts and grants over $100,000 meant FEMA could not pre-position Urban Search and Rescue teams without her approval. Authorization came more than 72 hours after flooding began.

**The Comfort counter-evidence:** A $60,000 siren system connected to river gauges gave Comfort residents time to act. Zero deaths. This is the cleanest data point in the entire dataset for what functional off-grid physical warning looks like — and what its absence costs.

1. **Girls in a tree, watching friends float past begging for help.** This is sourced from the lawsuit and the investigative report. It is specific, documented, and devastating.
2. **Comfort had $60,000 sirens. Kerr County had debates. Zero deaths vs. 137.** The contrast is surgical. Same storm, same river, different choice. This is the value proposition of off-grid warning made in blood.
3. **The 76-minute delay.** Director Eastland received the warning at 1:14 AM. He began evacuating at 2:30 AM. 27 people died. Everything that follows — the lawsuit, the report, the bankruptcy — begins with one hour and sixteen minutes.
4. **Camp Mystic girls had no phones.** The camp policy prohibited phones. Cell alerts — the primary county system — could not reach the most vulnerable population. Any solution that depends on personal devices fails for children, for rural residents without service, for anyone asleep.

# 6. TIBET EARTHQUAKE — January 7, 2025

## What Happened

A magnitude 7.1 earthquake struck Dingri County, Shigatse City, Tibet at **9:05 AM local time on January 7, 2025**, approximately 75 km northeast of Mount Everest. Focal depth was 10 km. Shaking was felt in Nepal, with tremors reaching Kathmandu.

At least **126 people killed, 188 injured**. More than **3,600 houses collapsed**. Villages in Dingri County were heavily damaged; the remote and high-altitude terrain (most of the county sits above 4,000 metres) made rescue access difficult in winter conditions.

About 1,500 fire and rescue workers and 200 soldiers were deployed. Rescuers pulled a trapped woman alive from rubble with bare hands and spades.

Monks from Sakya Monastery traveled for a full day and night to reach the 15 affected villages, holding memorial services. In some villages, residents embraced the monks and wept when they arrived.

**Context:** Dingri County is controlled by China. Media access was limited; independent reporting was not possible. Chinese state media provided the main casualty figures.

**Source:** [Wikipedia — 2025 Tibet Earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Tibet_earthquake) | [ABC News](https://abcnews.com/International/68-magnitude-earthquake-strikes-holy-city-tibet/story?id=117405149) | [International Campaign for Tibet](https://savetibet.org/tibet-earthquake-tibetan-resilience-and-future-risks/)

# 7. PHILIPPINES TYPHOON SEASON 2024 — Multiple Events

## What Happened

The Philippines experienced 17 tropical cyclones affecting the Philippine Area of Responsibility in 2024. The country ranked 7th most-affected globally by extreme weather in 2024.

**Major events:**

**Typhoon Yagi (local name: Enteng)** — September 2024. Made landfall in northern Luzon. 21 dead, 26 missing.

**Typhoon Gaemi (local name: Carina)** — mid-2024 peak season. Combined with Butchoy and the southwest monsoon. 48 dead. ₱12.38 billion in damages (~$210M USD).

**November 2024 cluster — 4 typhoons in a fortnight:**
- Typhoons Queenie, Rosita, Trami (Kristine), and Man-yi (Pepito) made landfall within two weeks

# SYNTHESIS: CROSS-EVENT ANALYSIS

## Three Most Powerful Honestly-Sourced Human Stories

### 1. The Camp Mystic Tree — Girls Watching Friends Float Past
**Event:** Texas Floods, July 4, 2025
**Source:** Texas House investigative report and lawsuits (CNN, FOX4 coverage)

Documented in the official investigative record and litigation: girls clinging to a tree in a flooding river in the dark, watching other campers being swept past and begging for help, unable to reach them. One girl was swept 6.5 miles downstream, pulled underwater multiple times, washed up on debris, and survived when two women heard her cries at dawn.

This is not composite. It is sourced. It is the consequence of: a 76-minute evacuation delay, a county that debated a $60,000 siren for years and never bought it, a camp policy that prohibited phones, a CodeRED system that required supervisor authorization at 4 AM, and a state with the highest flood-death rate in the US.

**Why it's powerful for a presentation:** It connects every failure in the chain to a single, specific, unbearable image. It has a counterpoint — 20 miles away, Comfort's sirens went off and nobody died.

### 2. The 8:11 PM Alert — Sent After the Deaths
**Event:** Valencia DANA, October 29, 2024
**Source:** Judicial investigation record, 1,000-page testimony report

The deaths happened between 7:00 and 8:00 PM. The warning was sent at 8:11 PM. This is not contested; the judge investigating the case has said it explicitly. The same meteorological data that prompted the Japanese embassy to warn its residents a full day in advance did not prompt the regional government to warn its own citizens until the deaths were complete.

**Why it's powerful:** It is the clearest documented case of a warning system that existed, had accurate data, and simply wasn't activated in time because of political decision-making. 238 dead. The technical problem was solved. The governance problem was not.

### 3. Traveling Three Days to Make a Phone Call
**Event:** Myanmar Earthquake, March 28, 2025
**Source:** Context/TRF, humanitarian worker account

In the junta-blackout zones of Sagaing — the epicenter — residents with urgent needs had no way to contact aid organizations. They walked. For days. To reach a town with connectivity, so they could describe what had happened and ask for help.

This is the absolute floor of the problem. Not a failed app, not a delayed cell alert, not a bureaucratic bottleneck — but a deliberate communications blackout maintained by a military dictatorship through a 7.7 earthquake because losing control of information was more threatening to the regime than letting people die.

**Why it's powerful:** It shows the extreme case. An off-grid solution — mesh networking, satellite-linked nodes, any physics-layer independent alternative — is not a nice-to-have in Myanmar. It is the difference between a community being able to summon help and a community that simply disappears.

## Which Events Support (or Undercut) an Off-Grid Comms + AI Triage Value Story

### Strongest Support

**Camp Mystic / Kerr County (Texas, 2025)** — The best-documented, most surgical case for the value of physical/off-grid early warning. Comfort had a $60,000 siren system and zero deaths. Kerr County had debates and 137 deaths. Cell alerts failed because: rural signal, sleeping users, and children with no phones. A system that doesn't depend on personal devices and cellular infrastructure is exactly what was needed. AI triage would have been relevant here — the NWS issued 22 accurate warnings; what failed was the translation of those warnings into action at the local level.

**Valencia DANA (Spain, 2024)** — Strong support for the AI coordination layer. The data existed. The warning models were accurate. What failed was governance — the decision of when to activate public alerts. An AI triage layer that could evaluate incoming meteorological data and automatically trigger ES-Alert messages (rather than waiting for political sign-off) would have changed outcomes. Volunteer coordination was also chaotic; a structured platform for real-time needs-matching was invented from scratch in the aftermath.

**Myanmar Earthquake (2025)** — The strongest case for off-grid mesh networking specifically. Every cellular and internet dependency failed simultaneously. Mesh radio, Starlink-equivalent hardware, any physics-independent local relay network would have been transformative. The "traveling three days to make a phone call" fact is the most compelling single data point for why off-grid doesn't mean "nice feature" — it means survivability in authoritarian or infrastructure-collapsed contexts.

**Wayanad (India, 2024)** — Good supporting case. A private ecology research center issued a warning 16 hours before the landslide that the district administration ignored because it wasn't integrated into the official system. An AI triage layer that aggregates signals from unofficial sources and flags them into official channels would have prevented this gap. The plantation workers who died had no connectivity; their survival was entirely word-of-mouth and physical self-evacuation.

### Partial Support / Honest Counterpoints

**Los Angeles Fires (2025)** — The pickleball WhatsApp case is actually a counterpoint to "you need complex off-grid technology": a simple consumer app saved lives because it was fast and informal. The failure was not technology — the technology existed. The failure was institutional: officials who had the data, the authority, and the tools did not use them in time. An off-grid solution doesn't fix that. What it might fix is the Rory Sykes case — a resident who couldn't self-evacuate, couldn't get help, and whose only hope was that someone with connectivity and response capacity reached him. That is an AI triage / first-responder routing problem.

**Philippines (2024)** — The story here is not one of technological failure but of resource exhaustion. Communities face 17 cyclones a year; they evacuate and return to the same flood-prone homes because they have nowhere else to go. Technology that warns them better doesn't address the underlying vulnerability. Honest counterpoint: in communities that are permanently at risk, warnings may just mean more orderly participation in recurring loss.

**Tibet (2025)** — Insufficient documentation for strong claims.

## For the Presentation: The Headline Value Story

The data supports this argument:

**Across every well-documented disaster from 2024–2026, the deaths that were preventable share one of two causes: (1) a warning that existed but wasn't delivered, or (2) a warning that was delivered to cell phones that weren't on, didn't have signal, or weren't allowed at the site.** The Comfort/Kerr comparison makes this argument in a single sentence: same storm, same river, one community had a $60,000 physical siren connected to a river gauge, zero people died. The other had a system that required supervisor authorization at 4 AM. 137 dead.

The off-grid + AI triage value proposition sits at the intersection of: local sensor triggers that don't wait for human authorization chains, physical/non-cellular broadcast that reaches people without phones, and an AI triage layer that translates sensor data and local reports into coordinated response recommendations. Myanmar is the extreme case. Kerr County is the domestic policy case. Valencia is the governance failure case. They all point to the same gap.
